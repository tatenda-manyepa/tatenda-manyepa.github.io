1. Business Problem

The organisation is experiencing recurring operational instability within its fulfilment platform, including high-severity outages impacting robotic fulfilment operations, inventory allocation, and order processing workflows. These incidents have resulted in operational disruption, delayed customer deliveries, increased pressure on support teams, and reduced confidence in platform resilience.

The current technology landscape is heavily dependent on legacy on-premises infrastructure with limited scalability, manually managed high availability processes, and minimal operational observability. Support teams also operate across siloed structures, creating inconsistent troubleshooting approaches and heavy reliance on individual technical knowledge.

Operational Stability Issues

Recurring high-severity outages disrupt robotic movement, stock allocation, and order picking processes across fulfilment operations.

Limited High Availability & DR

Manual standby infrastructure and limited automated failover capability increase recovery times and create single points of failure.

Reactive Support Model

Issues are typically identified after service degradation or failure, with limited proactive alerting and slow root cause analysis.

Siloed Teams & Knowledge Gaps

Development, testing, and support operate independently, creating poor cross-team visibility and inconsistent troubleshooting processes.

On-Premises Infrastructure Constraints

Database timeouts, network instability, maintenance overhead, and limited horizontal scalability affect peak operational periods.

Deployment & Change Risk

Hotfixes and changes require stronger CI/CD controls, validation, rollback planning, and post-release monitoring.

6. Architecture Decision Records

Architecture is about making informed trade-offs. These decision records explain the key choices behind the target-state architecture, including the options considered, selected approach, benefits and trade-offs.

Business Challenge

The current fulfilment platform relies on manually managed infrastructure, reactive recovery and significant maintenance overhead.

Options Considered

  • Remain on-premise
  • Hybrid cloud
  • AWS-first architecture

Decision

Adopt an AWS-first architecture using managed services where practical, while maintaining secure integration with operational technology systems.

Benefits

  • Reduced operational overhead
  • Improved scalability and resilience
  • Better automation and observability
  • Faster recovery patterns

Trade-Offs

AWS reduces infrastructure ownership but introduces ongoing cloud operating costs, AWS skills requirements and some provider dependency. This is mitigated through governance, cost controls, containerisation and clear architecture decision records.

Business Challenge

Operational issues are often detected after business impact has already occurred, with investigations spread across logs, databases and support knowledge.

Options Considered

  • Basic monitoring only
  • Third-party monitoring platform
  • AWS-native observability stack

Decision

Embed observability into the target architecture using CloudWatch, OpenSearch, SNS alerts, dashboards and Systems Manager runbooks.

Benefits

  • Faster incident detection
  • Improved root cause analysis
  • Reduced MTTR
  • Centralised operational visibility

Trade-Offs

Centralised observability increases log volume, storage costs and alert management effort. To control this, the design requires retention policies, dashboard ownership, alert tuning and continuous operational review.

Business Challenge

The current platform depends on traditional application servers that require patching, manual deployment, capacity planning and operational recovery.

Options Considered

  • Continue with traditional servers
  • Self-managed containers on EC2
  • ECS Fargate managed container platform

Decision

Use ECS Fargate for containerised application services across multiple Availability Zones.

Benefits

  • No EC2 server management
  • Improved scaling and deployment consistency
  • Foundation for CI/CD and rollback
  • Reduced operational burden

Trade-Offs

Some legacy services may require refactoring before containerisation. Teams also need to learn container operations, task definitions, service scaling and deployment patterns. This is accepted because it reduces long-term server management risk.

Business Challenge

The current state contains single points of failure and relies heavily on manual failover and individual operational knowledge.

Options Considered

  • Single Availability Zone
  • Traditional active/passive server failover
  • Multi-AZ AWS architecture

Decision

Deploy critical application, database and messaging components across multiple Availability Zones.

Benefits

  • Reduced single points of failure
  • Improved availability
  • Better business continuity
  • Reduced manual recovery dependency

Trade-Offs

Multi-AZ design increases cost and requires resilience testing. Applications must be designed and validated for failover scenarios. This cost is justified because fulfilment downtime has direct operational and customer impact.

Business Challenge

Support engineers spend significant time searching tickets, runbooks, documentation and logs during incidents. Knowledge is often siloed with experienced engineers.

Options Considered

  • Traditional documentation only
  • Centralised knowledge search
  • AI-assisted operations assistant

Decision

Use an AI Operations Intelligence layer with S3 Knowledge Store, Bedrock Knowledge Base, OpenSearch Vector Index and an AI Operations Assistant.

Benefits

  • Faster troubleshooting
  • Improved knowledge retention
  • Consistent investigation guidance
  • Reduced dependency on individual experts

Trade-Offs

AI guidance depends on the quality of the knowledge base and must be validated by engineers before operational action. The platform also requires governance, review cycles and maintenance of source documentation.
